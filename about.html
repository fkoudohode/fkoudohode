Who I am

My name is Florent Koudohode. I grew up in Benin, where a single course in differential equations at the Institut de Mathématiques et de Sciences Physiques (IMSP) made me suspect that there was a hidden language behind every physical phenomenon I had ever noticed — and that I wanted to learn it.

That suspicion eventually carried me to Toulouse, where I completed a Master and a PhD at LAAS-CNRS under the guidance of Lucie Baudouin and Sophie Tarbouriech, working on event-based control of partial differential equations. Today I am a postdoctoral researcher at the Technical University of Crete, supported by the European Research Council on the C-NORA project, where I think about how to stabilise infinite-dimensional systems when the world insists on being delayed, quantized and noisy.

What I love most about this work is that it sits exactly at the seam between pure mathematics and the messy reality of engineering — Lyapunov functionals on one side, sensors and actuators on the other, and a real chance to make both speak to each other. Outside the lab, you'll find me playing amateur rugby with the Cretan Gunners or trying — with mixed success — to learn Greek.

Short biography

For invitations, talks, journal headers

Florent Koudohode is a Postdoctoral Researcher in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the Technical University of Crete, Greece, supported by the European Research Council under the 2022 Consolidator Grant C-NORA (#101088147). His research focuses on the analysis and control of infinite-dimensional systems governed by partial differential equations, with particular emphasis on event-triggered boundary control, predictor feedback for delayed systems, and state and input quantization. He received his Ph.D. in Automatic Control from Université Paul Sabatier — Toulouse III (LAAS-CNRS) in 2023, under the supervision of Lucie Baudouin and Sophie Tarbouriech. His work has been published in leading journals including Automatica, Systems & Control Letters and IEEE Transactions on Automatic Control, and he collaborates with researchers such as Miroslav Krstic (UC San Diego) and Nicolás Espitia (CNRS, Université de Lille).
